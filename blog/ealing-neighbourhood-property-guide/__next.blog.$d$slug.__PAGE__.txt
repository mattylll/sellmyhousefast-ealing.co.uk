With an overall average house price of £560,000, Ealing sits above the Greater London average — but that headline figure tells only part of the story.</p><p>Within the borough, prices range from under £250,000 for a flat in Northolt to well over £1 million for a detached family home near Ealing Common. This guide breaks down what makes each neighbourhood unique, the property types you will find there, and what sellers in each area need to know.</p><h2>Ealing Broadway and Pitshanger</h2><p>Ealing Broadway is the borough's flagship neighbourhood — the area that earns Ealing its reputation as the 'Queen of the Suburbs'. The residential streets surrounding Walpole Park and Pitzhanger Manor feature some of the finest period architecture in West London: substantial Victorian and Edwardian houses, many with original features, generous gardens, and mature tree-lined settings.</p><p>Property prices here are the highest in the borough, with four-bedroom houses regularly exceeding £800,000 and the best properties breaking the £1 million barrier. Pitshanger Lane, a short walk from the Broadway, has developed into one of London's best independent high streets, with cafes, restaurants, and boutiques that add significant lifestyle appeal.</p><p>The Elizabeth line station at Ealing Broadway provides direct services to Paddington in 10 minutes and Liverpool Street in under 30 minutes. This connectivity, combined with the area's character and amenities, supports strong and consistent demand from affluent families and professionals.</p><p>For sellers, the premium market has its own dynamics. While demand is solid, the smaller pool of buyers at this price point means longer marketing periods — six months or more is not unusual for properties above £700,000. Chain risks are elevated because buyers at this level often have complex arrangements. Sellers who need certainty may find a cash buyer the most practical route to completing a sale in this bracket.</p><h2>Acton</h2><p>Acton sits at the borough's eastern edge and has experienced perhaps the most dramatic transformation of any Ealing neighbourhood. Once regarded as affordable and slightly rough around the edges, Acton has gentrified rapidly, driven by the Elizabeth line, new residential developments, and proximity to Chiswick and Shepherd's Bush.</p><p>The housing stock spans Victorian terraces in central Acton, inter-war semis in West Acton, modern apartments in the South Acton regeneration zone, and mansion flats near Acton Green. Prices range from around £350,000 for smaller flats to over £700,000 for family homes near the Chiswick border.</p><p>The area appeals to a younger demographic — professionals and couples attracted by transport links and the growing food and drink scene along Churchfield Road. Investor activity has also been significant, drawn by strong rental demand and capital growth prospects.</p><p>Sellers in Acton face competition from new-build developments, which attract buyers with incentives and modern specifications. Properties that need updating can take longer to sell in this environment. However, the strong overall demand means that well-priced properties continue to find buyers. For those who need speed or certainty, cash buyers offer a guaranteed outcome in a market that can be competitive and unpredictable.</p><h2>Hanwell</h2><p>Hanwell occupies a comfortable middle ground within the borough: more affordable than Ealing Broadway but with a stronger community identity than some of the areas further west. The neighbourhood straddles the River Brent and is defined by its Edwardian and inter-war housing stock — rows of terraced and semi-detached houses with the character and space that families seek.</p><p>The Wharncliffe Viaduct, a Grade I listed structure carrying the GWR line over the Brent Valley, is a local landmark that gives Hanwell a distinctive sense of place. The Elizabeth line station at Hanwell has improved connectivity significantly, bringing the area within easy reach of Central London.</p><p>Property prices in Hanwell typically range from £450,000 to £600,000 for houses, with flats available from around £300,000. The buyer profile is predominantly young families, many of whom are trading up from flats in more central locations. This creates a healthy market but one that can be disrupted by chain complications, as buyers often have their own properties to sell.</p><p>Sellers in Hanwell generally experience a reasonable market, though dated properties or those requiring significant work may sit longer than move-in-ready homes. A cash sale can be particularly useful for inherited properties or homes that have not been updated in years.</p><h2>Southall</h2><p>Southall is unlike anywhere else in the London Borough of Ealing — or indeed in London. The neighbourhood's South Asian heritage gives it a cultural richness and vibrancy that is unique in West London. Southall Market, the restaurants along The Broadway, and community landmarks like the Gurdwara Sri Guru Singh Sabha create an atmosphere that draws visitors from across the capital.</p><p>The property market in Southall is in a period of significant change. The Elizabeth line has brought transformative connectivity improvements, and major regeneration schemes are bringing new residential and commercial development to the area. Traditional housing stock — Victorian terraces, inter-war houses, and converted properties — sits alongside emerging new-build developments.</p><p>Prices in Southall remain below the borough average, making the area one of the more affordable parts of Ealing. This affordability, combined with improving infrastructure, creates an interesting dynamic for sellers. Values are rising, but the influx of new-build supply means that older properties face competition for buyer attention.</p><p>The complexity of Southall's market — including a high proportion of HMOs, multi-generational ownership structures, and properties requiring renovation — makes it an area where cash buyers can add particular value. We purchase properties in any condition and any ownership situation, providing a straightforward route to sale.</p><h2>Northolt</h2><p>Northolt represents Ealing at its most suburban and most affordable. Located at the western edge of the borough, it offers inter-war and post-war housing with the space and greenery that characterise outer London, along with Central line tube access that provides a direct route to Central London.</p><p>Property prices here are the lowest in the borough, making Northolt a key market for first-time buyers and families seeking value. Semi-detached houses, the area's most common property type, typically sell between £400,000 and £500,000. Ex-council properties offer even more affordable options.</p><p>For sellers, Northolt's price-sensitive market means that pricing accuracy is critical. Overvalued properties sit on the market, while competitively priced homes in good condition can sell relatively quickly. Cash buyers are a strong option here for properties in poor condition or with leasehold complications that deter mortgage-dependent buyers.</p><h2>Greenford</h2><p>Greenford occupies the central-western portion of the borough, offering a blend of suburban character and decent transport connections via the Central line and GWR services. The housing stock is predominantly inter-war — classic 1930s semi-detached houses that give the area its recognisable streetscape.</p><p>Prices sit in the borough's mid-range, with houses typically between £450,000 and £550,000. The area attracts families and established households who value space, schools, and the accessible green spaces including Horsenden Hill, one of the highest points in the borough.</p><p>The market in Greenford is steady rather than dynamic, with moderate transaction volumes and gradual price growth. Sellers can expect a reasonable selling experience if priced correctly, but the area's moderate buyer activity means that any friction — poor presentation, condition issues, or pricing errors — can significantly extend marketing times.</p><h2>Perivale</h2><p>Perivale is a compact neighbourhood between Greenford and Ealing, defined by its inter-war housing stock, Central line station, and the iconic Art Deco Hoover Building. The residential streets are quiet and well-maintained, with semi-detached houses being the dominant property type.</p><p>The relatively small size of Perivale means fewer transactions and a thinner comparable sales dataset, which can make pricing challenging. Properties typically sell between £450,000 and £550,000, and the area appeals to a similar demographic as Greenford — families and commuters seeking suburban calm with tube access.</p><h2>Making the Right Decision</h2><p>Whatever neighbourhood your property sits in, understanding the specific local dynamics is essential for choosing the best selling route.
